## SECTION B1 — The Old City & Heritage

Context for the guide, not a booking itinerary. All entries cross-checked against Wikipedia. Confidence: **High** unless noted.

- **Umayyad Mosque** — Completed in 715 CE by Caliph al-Walid I on the site of a Roman Temple of Jupiter (earlier still a Byzantine cathedral). One of the oldest and largest mosques in the world; its basilical plan and gold mosaics shaped mosque architecture across the Islamic world, and it remains the spiritual and civic heart of the city today.
- **Souq al-Hamidiyya** — Damascus's great covered market, built from around 1780 under Ottoman sultans Abdul Hamid I and II. It runs roughly 600 meters along the line of an old Roman colonnaded road, straight toward the mosque — and it's the address for Bakdash and three other picks above.
- **Straight Street (Via Recta)** — The city's Roman/Seleucid-era main street, "the street called Straight" of the Book of Acts, where Saint Paul is said to have stayed and later escaped the city. Today it's split into Midhat Pasha Street (souq, western half) and Bab Sharqi Street (eastern half, toward the Eastern Gate) — Old Damascus Snack sits directly on it.
- **Azem Palace** — An 18th-century Ottoman governor's residence, built in 1749 for As'ad Pasha al-Azm, in the black-and-white banded stone (ablaq) typical of high Damascene style. Now the Museum of Arts and Popular Traditions.
- **Old Damascene courtyard houses** — The Old City's signature domestic architecture: a plain street wall hiding a private courtyard, fountain, and citrus trees within. Several of the finest surviving examples — including two on our featured list, Beit Jabri and Al Qishla Café — have been carefully turned into restaurants and cafés rather than left empty, which is as good a way as any to actually experience one.
- **Mount Qasioun** — The hill overlooking the whole city (1,151m), lined with view restaurants and layered in local lore, including the Cave of the Seven Sleepers referenced in both early Christian sources and the Quran.
- **Maaloula & Saidnaya** — Two mountain towns near Damascus, often mentioned in the same breath as the capital's heritage circuit. Maaloula (~56km northeast) is one of the last places where Western Neo-Aramaic — related to the language Jesus is believed to have spoken — is still an everyday tongue, home to the ancient Mar Sarkis and Saint Thecla monasteries. Saidnaya (~27km north) centers on the Our Lady of Saidnaya monastery, a major Christian pilgrimage site tradition holds was founded in 547 CE. Noted here as heritage context only — not part of any itinerary in this guide, and access/safety at either site would need an independent, current check before anyone acted on it.

Damascus is also commonly described as one of the oldest continuously inhabited cities in the world and is sometimes called the oldest continuously serving capital — a claim it shares with a small number of other ancient settlements rather than holding outright. **Confidence: Medium** on the superlative framing (it's a genuinely contested title across several cities), **High** that Damascus is a legitimate, first-rank claimant.

## SECTION B2 — Damascene Food Culture

Eight signature dishes and experiences, each traceable to at least one pick in Section A.

1. **Damascene mezze** — the ritual of small shared plates (hummus, mutabbal/baba ghanoush, fattoush, kibbeh nayyeh) that opens a real meal — built for a table of family and friends, not a solo diner.
2. **Kibbeh** — bulgur worked with minced lamb and spices into torpedoes, patties, or baked trays; Syria's most-cited signature dish and a point of real regional pride and rivalry.
3. **Fatteh** — a layered dish of toasted or fried flatbread under yogurt or tahini, chickpeas, or chicken — a Damascus breakfast or shared starter, on the menu at Beit Jabri among others.
4. **Shawarma** — meat shaved from a vertical spit into fresh bread; a Damascus street-food backbone long before it became a global export, still judged locally by the spit and the bread, not the sauce.
5. **Bakdash booza** — Damascus's own frozen dessert: mastic and salep (ground orchid root) worked by hand until it stretches and resists melting, pounded to order in full view of the queue in the souq.
6. **Knafeh & the Damascus sweets counter** — shredded pastry or semolina over soft cheese, soaked in syrup, alongside mabrouma, baklawa and other counter sweets from old confectionery houses (Ghraoui is among the Damascus names known internationally, though we couldn't independently verify its house history this session) — a tradition sweet-makers here were already refining under the Umayyads.
7. **Old-city coffeehouses & narghile** — cafés like Al-Nawfara, built around slow tea or coffee and a water pipe, historically also home to the hakawati — a professional storyteller reciting serialized tales from an old leather-bound book to a room of regulars.
8. **Damascus rose** — the damask rose (Rosa × damascena) takes its Western name from the city and runs through the food culture as rosewater in syrups, sweets, and drinks — a scent that's as much "Damascus" as any single dish.
