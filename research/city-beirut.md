# SECTION B — Traveler Essentials (web research)

## B1. Best neighborhoods for visitors

| Neighborhood | Character | Who it suits |
|---|---|---|
| **Hamra** | Bustling commercial/cultural spine of Ras Beirut, anchored by the American University of Beirut (AUB) — cafés, bookshops, restaurants, dense and "very real," busy nearly any hour. *(High confidence)* | First-timers and budget-to-mid travelers who want a central, walkable, good-value base with the widest food scene overlap with Section A. |
| **Mar Mikhael** | Seaside district of eastern Beirut bordering Gemmayzeh; Beirut's most vibrant nightlife strip — Armenia Street turns into an open-air bar scene after dark. *(High confidence)* | Nightlife-seekers and younger travelers comfortable with noise; less ideal for an early-night stay. |
| **Gemmayzeh** | Bars, restaurants and shops in colorful French-mandate-era buildings; street art, a cat café, intimate lounges; connects to Mar Mikhael via the Saint Nicolas Stairs. *(High confidence)* | Design/atmosphere lovers who want charm and nightlife access without being directly on the loudest strip. |
| **Achrafieh** | Beirut's most elegant, hillside residential district — red-roofed old houses next to modern towers, the Sursock Museum, and boutique hotels clustered near Monnot Street. *(High confidence)* | Couples and culture-focused travelers wanting a quieter, upscale "chic village" base. |
| **Badaro** | Tree-lined and increasingly hip, home to the National Museum and bordering Horsh Beirut park; good by day (café culture) and by night. *(High confidence)* | Families and travelers in their 30s+ who want a bohemian-but-comfortable, less touristy base. |
| **Downtown** | Rebuilt in a "Paris of the Middle East" style — Beirut Souks, Martyrs' Square, a grand mosque, and luxury brands (Chanel, Hermès); polished and quiet compared to the rest of the city. *(High confidence)* | Shoppers, luxury travelers, and business visitors who want polish and easy walkability to sights; least "local" texture of the six. |

## B4. Signature food — 4–6 must-eats

1. **Manoushe** — the definitive Lebanese breakfast flatbread, topped with za'atar, cheese, or spiced meat, baked fresh; best straight from a stone-oven bakery (Section A: Abu Shadi, Man'oushit Chaiban). *(High confidence)*
2. **Foul & hummus breakfast** — fava beans, chickpeas, olive oil, a genuine daily ritual rather than a tourist dish; our own dataset alone has half a dozen dedicated counters (Abou Abdallah, Abu Hassan, Malek al Foul, Le Professeur, Issa). *(High confidence)*
3. **Mezze spread** — hummus, moutabal/baba ghanoush, tabbouleh, fattoush, kibbeh, stuffed vine leaves, shared family-style across the table. *(High confidence)*
4. **Knefeh** — semolina-and-cheese pastry soaked in syrup, eaten as a sweet breakfast or dessert; for a vetted stop, our dataset's Hamdi Al Zahabi Sweets serves it alongside baklava and halawet el jibn. *(High confidence on the dish; didn't independently verify a single "best in Beirut" knefeh spot from web sources, so we're pointing to our own vetted pick instead)*
5. **Lebanese wine** — the Bekaa Valley is the historic wine region, home to long-established producers (Château Ksara, Château Kefraya, Massaya, Domaine des Tourelles, IXSIR among the best-known). *(Medium confidence — general knowledge of the industry, not freshly re-verified tasting-room status for 2026; confirm hours before a winery day trip)*
6. **Armenian food in Bourj Hammoud** — basterma (cured beef), soujouk (spiced sausage), mante, itch, kebbe — a distinct culinary layer of the city, directly reinforced by our own data (Basterma Mano, Resto Ghazar, Varouj, Apo, Falafel Arax all sit in Bourj Hammoud). *(High confidence)*

## B5. Getting there / around — brief and factual

- **Airport**: Beirut–Rafic Hariri International Airport (BEY) is Lebanon's sole airport, south of the city near the southern suburbs — roughly 15–25 minutes to Hamra/Downtown/Achrafieh depending on traffic. *(High confidence)*
- **From the airport**: official taxi counters inside arrivals are the simplest first-timer option — agree the fare before getting in. Bolt also operates in Beirut (metered in-app fare, no haggling), though airport security has reportedly turned away some Bolt pickups that arrive without an already-matched passenger, so it can be hit-or-miss right at arrivals. *(Medium-High confidence on taxi counters; Medium confidence on the Bolt-at-airport friction, based on one source)*
- **Around town**: neighborhoods are walkable internally, but sidewalks between districts are patchy or absent and distances can be deceptive — a short Bolt/taxi hop between neighborhoods (e.g., Hamra to Mar Mikhael) is normal, cheap, and expected. *(Medium-High confidence)*
- **Currency & cash**: the Lebanese pound has been holding roughly steady at ~89,000–90,000 LBP/USD through mid-2026 *(High confidence — live exchange-rate data)*, even as annual inflation is still running high, around 17–19% year-on-year as of Q2 2026 *(High confidence, multiple sources agree)*. In practice, Beirut has run on a cash-and-USD basis since the 2019–2020 banking crisis: crisp, newer US bills are the practical currency for hotels, restaurants, taxis and tours; card acceptance is inconsistent outside upscale venues; ATM withdrawals (especially in USD, especially on foreign cards) can be unreliable. Bring more small-denomination USD cash than you think you'll need. *(Medium confidence — this reflects well-documented multi-year practice plus one direct 2026 source; we didn't re-verify card acceptance venue-by-venue, so treat as directional and recheck close to your travel date)*
- **Power**: grid electricity is commonly supplemented by neighborhood generator subscriptions; hotels and restaurants generally handle this seamlessly — a real feature of daily life, not something to be alarmed by. *(Medium confidence)*
- **Safety — official guidance, not our own risk judgment**: as of this research date, the U.S. State Department places Lebanon at **Level 4 – Do Not Travel**, citing crime, terrorism, civil unrest, kidnapping and limited emergency-assistance capacity; a Feb 2026 order directed non-emergency U.S. embassy personnel to depart. The UK FCDO advises against **all travel** to specific areas — the South and Nabatiyeh, Beqaa, and Baalbek-Hermel governorates, parts of Tripoli, the Palestinian refugee camps, and some southern Beirut suburbs — and against **all but essential travel** to the rest of Beirut and Mount Lebanon governorate. A cessation of hostilities was announced 16 April 2026, but both agencies describe the situation as fluid, with continued airstrikes and military presence concentrated in southern Lebanon near the Israeli border, well outside the neighborhoods covered in this brief. *(High confidence — pulled directly from the State Department advisory aggregator and the UK government's own FCDO page today)* **This brief does not make an independent safety call.** Check the current, dated advisory at travel.state.gov and gov.uk/foreign-travel-advice before booking or traveling, and consider registering with your embassy.

## B6. Best time to visit

- **Spring (April–June) and autumn (September–October)** — warm, dry, comfortable (roughly 15–28°C) — the classic sweet-spot shoulder seasons for both the coast and day trips. *(High confidence)*
- **Summer (July–August)** — hot on the coast, and the busiest/most expensive season (diaspora summer-return crowds inflate hotel prices and restaurant waits). *(High confidence)*
- **Winter (December–February)** — mild by regional standards (~14°C average in January) with occasional rain, wind and storms; also the cheapest hotel season alongside November and March. *(Medium-High confidence)*
- Lebanon is often described as the only Levant country with genuine four-season variation — a mild coast within an hour of snow in the mountains. *(Medium confidence — a commonly repeated claim, not independently fact-checked further here)*
