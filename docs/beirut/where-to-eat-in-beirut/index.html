Where to Eat in Beirut: A Local Food Guide

Fifteen places worth crossing the city for, the Lebanese dishes you have to order, and which neighborhood to eat in — from a hummus counter unchanged since 1965 to a two-seating chef's table.

Beirut eats before it does almost anything else. This is a city where a hundred-year-old falafel counter and a two-seating chef's table sit four kilometers apart, where breakfast is a genuine destination, and where the table is still — whatever else is happening around it — the center of everything. Here's where to go, ranked.

15 places worth crossing the city for

This list runs from a 1965 hummus counter to a modern chef's table, chosen for being genuinely excellent and genuinely Beiruti — not for being easy to find. All fifteen have real menus, real regulars and real reviews behind them.

Mar Mikhael

LMATBAKH

Chefs Christine Saad and Elie Harb cook a modern Lebanese tasting menu live at the counter in front of you, over two nightly seatings only. Dine-in only, and you'll want to book ahead — this is Beirut's most talked-about table right now.

Six dishes to build a trip around

Breakfast flatbread

Manoushe

Za'atar, cheese or spiced meat baked fresh on a stone oven — the definitive Lebanese breakfast. Abu Shadi's spinach-and-cheese version, behind an unmarked Hamra storefront, is one of the best.

The morning ritual

Foul & hummus

Fava beans and chickpeas, olive oil, and a genuine daily habit rather than a tourist dish. Abou Abdallah, Abu Hassan and Malek al Foul are three completely different doors into the same tradition.

Any evening

Mezze spread

Hummus, moutabal, tabbouleh, fattoush, kibbeh, stuffed vine leaves — shared, family-style, across the whole table. Order too much on purpose.

Sweet, warm, syrup-soaked

Knefeh

Semolina-and-cheese pastry soaked in syrup, eaten as a sweet breakfast or dessert. Hamdi Al Zahabi Sweets serves it fresh off the tray alongside baklava and halawet el jibn.

Bekaa Valley

Lebanese wine

The Bekaa Valley is one of the world's oldest wine regions — look for bottles from Château Ksara, Château Kefraya, Massaya, Domaine des Tourelles or IXSIR on a Beirut wine list.

A different Beirut entirely

Armenian Bourj Hammoud

Basterma, soujouk, mante, kebbe — Bourj Hammoud's Armenian cooking is its own culinary layer. Beyond Basterma Mano and Resto Ghazar above, look for Apo (Armenian BBQ since 1976), Restaurant Varouj (45-plus years, no menu — you eat what Varouj cooks) and Falafel Arax (Armenian falafel since 1949).

☼ Eating out, practically

Most of the places on this list are cash-only, family-run counters — bring small US bills, since card acceptance is inconsistent outside the fancier spots. A few keep unusual hours worth planning around: Abu Hassan never closes, Abou Abdallah opens at 6am, and Zmrd Social Space is closed on Sundays. Come hungry, and don't rush the table — that's rather the whole point.

Frequently asked questions

What's the best neighborhood to eat in Beirut?

There isn't one — that's the fun of it. Hamra has the breakfast institutions and hummus counters, Mar Mikhael and Gemmayzeh have the modern kitchens and grills, Achrafieh has the sweets, and Bourj Hammoud has an entire Armenian food culture you won't find anywhere else in the city. Plan to hit at least three.

Is street food safe to eat in Beirut?

Generally yes, by the usual rule: go where the queue and the turnover are both high. Places like Abu Hassan and Falafel Tabbara have been run by the same families for decades and are trusted by locals every single day — that track record, plus food made fresh in front of you, is the best signal you'll get.

Do I need cash to eat out in Beirut?

Mostly, yes. Most of the counters and family-run spots on this list are cash-only, and Beirut has operated on a cash-and-US-dollar basis since the 2019–2020 banking crisis. Bring small, crisp USD bills — card acceptance is inconsistent outside the more upscale restaurants.
