Beirut

Loud, warm, and one of the great eating cities of the Mediterranean. The neighborhoods, the tables worth crossing town for, and how to visit thoughtfully.

Beirut eats before it does almost anything else. This is a city where a hundred-year-old falafel counter and a two-seating chef's table sit four kilometers apart, where breakfast is a genuine destination, and where the table is still — whatever else is happening around it — the center of everything. Here's how to eat and move through it well.

Lebanese food doesn't really have a 'scene' so much as a rhythm: foul and hummus at dawn, manoushe mid-morning, mezze stretching across a whole evening, sweets whenever. Beirut does all of it, spread across neighborhoods with sharply different personalities. Hamra, Ras Beirut's bustling commercial spine around the American University of Beirut, is dense with breakfast counters and bookshop cafés and rarely quiet. Mar Mikhael, down by the water, turns into Beirut's liveliest bar strip after dark, its restaurants and grills feeding the same crowd. Gemmayzeh, one flight of stairs away — literally, the Saint Nicolas Stairs connect them — fills French-mandate-era buildings with bars, street art and a slightly calmer version of the same energy. Up the hill, Achrafieh is Beirut's elegant, red-roofed quarter, home to sweets shops and quieter, more refined tables. And across town, Bourj Hammoud — settled by Armenian refugees in the early twentieth century and still the heart of Armenian Beirut — has an entirely distinct food identity: basterma, soujouk, kebbe, a whole cured-meat and mezze culture you won't find in the same form anywhere else in the city.

CH. 03

What to see

Corniche & Pigeon Rocks Free, self-guided

A 4.8km seafront promenade for walking, jogging or watching the sunset — follow it out to Raouché for a free view of the Pigeon Rocks, Beirut's only natural sea arches.

Beirut National Museum Badaro

About 1,300 Phoenician-to-Mamluk artifacts, including the Byblos figurines — plus the quietly remarkable story of how staff protected the collection through the civil war.

Sursock Museum Achrafieh

Modern and contemporary art across 800-plus works in a grand Achrafieh building, with the still-private Sursock Palace visible right across the street.

Beirut Souks & Downtown Martyrs' Square

The rebuilt heart of the city — 200-plus shops in a polished, walkable streetscape, and a good bookend to a Downtown afternoon.

Jeita Grotto, Byblos & Harissa Day trip

The single most popular day out of Beirut: a vast cave system, the Crusader-era harbor town of Byblos, and a cable car up to the Harissa statue.

CH. 04

Getting there & around

Beirut–Rafic Hariri International (BEY) is Lebanon's only airport, about 15–25 minutes from Hamra, Downtown or Achrafieh depending on traffic. The official taxi counters inside arrivals are the simplest first move — agree the fare before you get in. Bolt also operates across the city with metered, no-haggling fares, though it can be hit-or-miss picking up right at the airport.

  • Around town: each neighborhood is walkable in itself, but sidewalks between them are patchy, so a short Bolt or taxi hop — Hamra to Mar Mikhael, say — is normal and cheap. Don't assume you can just walk it.
  • Cash rules here. Beirut has run on a cash-and-USD basis since the 2019–2020 banking crisis. Bring more small, crisp US bills than you think you'll need — card acceptance is inconsistent outside upscale places, and ATMs can be unreliable.
  • Power cuts are normal. Grid electricity is widely supplemented by neighborhood generator subscriptions; hotels and restaurants handle it seamlessly and it isn't something to worry about.
⚠ Before you go

Lebanon currently carries formal government travel advisories, and they're worth taking seriously — but they aren't uniform. Several governments currently draw a real distinction between Beirut and Mount Lebanon, generally urging caution there, and the south and border areas, which carry stronger warnings; a cessation of hostilities announced in spring 2026 has held, though both the US and UK describe the situation as fluid. Advisories are updated often and differ by country, so treat this paragraph as a starting point, not the final word. Check your own government's current, dated advisory — travel.state.gov for US citizens, gov.uk/foreign-travel-advice for UK citizens — before you book, and again closer to your trip, and consider registering with your embassy once you're there.

☼ Best time to visit

April–June and September–October are the sweet spot — warm, dry and comfortable, good for both the coast and day trips. July–August is hot and the busiest, priciest season, as the diaspora comes home for summer. Winter is mild by regional standards, with occasional storms, and the cheapest hotel rates alongside November and March. Lebanon is often called the only country in the Levant with genuine four-season variation — a mild coast within an hour of snow in the mountains.

Beirut FAQ

Is it safe to travel to Beirut right now?

It depends on your own government's current advisory, which is exactly why we won't give you a flat yes or no here. Several governments currently distinguish between Beirut and Mount Lebanon — generally urging caution rather than an outright ban — and the country's south and border areas, which carry stronger warnings. Check the current, dated advisory at travel.state.gov or gov.uk/foreign-travel-advice before you book, and again closer to your trip.

How many days do you need in Beirut?

Three or four days is enough to work through several neighborhoods and eat very well. Add a day if you want the Jeita Grotto–Byblos–Harissa day trip, the single most popular excursion out of the city.

What should I eat first in Beirut?

Start where Beirut itself starts: a foul-and-hummus breakfast at a counter like Abou Abdallah or Malek al Foul, then a manoushe an hour later because you'll want one. Our full food guide has the rest, ranked.
