Best Things to Do in Beirut

The sea promenade, the museums, the blue-domed mosque downtown, and the day trip north worth building an extra day around.

You could spend a week in Beirut without running out of things worth doing, but you don't need a week to hit the essentials. This is the honest shortlist — the sights that earn a half-day each, the neighborhood that rewards just wandering, and the one day trip worth building an extra day around.

The sights worth your time

Beirut's essential sights cluster into two very different modes: a seafront-and-downtown day of museums and landmarks, and a completely different afternoon across town in Bourj Hammoud. Start with these five:

Corniche & Pigeon Rocks Free, self-guided

Beirut's 4.8km seafront promenade, best walked at golden hour with the whole city out for its evening stroll. Follow it west toward Raouché for a free clifftop view of the Pigeon Rocks, the country's only natural sea arches.

Beirut National Museum Badaro

Around 1,300 artifacts running from Phoenician to Mamluk Beirut, including the Byblos figurines — plus the quietly remarkable story of how museum staff hid and protected the collection through the civil war. Self-ticketed at the door.

Sursock Museum Achrafieh

Modern and contemporary art across 800-plus works, inside a grand Achrafieh building — with the still-private, gated Sursock Palace visible right across the street. Self-ticketed.

Beirut Souks & Downtown Martyrs' Square

The rebuilt heart of the city — 200-plus shops in a polished, walkable streetscape around Martyrs' Square. Free to walk, and easy to pair with the mosque next door.

Mohammad Al-Amin Mosque Downtown

Downtown's blue-domed landmark, finished in 2008 and hard to miss from Martyrs' Square — it stands right beside the Maronite Cathedral of Saint George, a pairing locals point to as a symbol of the city's coexistence.

Bourj Hammoud: an afternoon of Armenian Beirut

Across town from the sights above, Bourj Hammoud is a different Beirut entirely — dense market streets, Armenian churches, and cured-meat and mezze shops with no real equivalent elsewhere in the city. There's no ticket and no tour required, just wander. Time it around lunch and pair it with our food guide's Bourj Hammoud picks — Basterma Mano, Resto Ghazar, Varouj and Apo are all a short walk from each other.

Day trip north: Jeita, Byblos & Harissa

The single most popular excursion out of Beirut strings together three completely different stops within about an hour's drive: a vast limestone cave system, one of the world's oldest continuously inhabited port towns, and a mountaintop statue reached by cable car.

Jeita Grotto, Byblos & Harissa The classic day trip

Jeita Grotto is a two-level cave system and a former New7Wonders of Nature finalist. Byblos is a Crusader-era harbor town built over thousands of years of continuous settlement, castle included. Harissa caps the day with a cable-car ride up to the 'Our Lady of Lebanon' statue and a view back down over the coast. Most operators run all three together as one full-day tour.

⚠ Before you go

Lebanon carries formal government travel advisories that vary by country and change often, so this isn't an independent safety call. Everything above — including the day trip north — sits within Beirut and Mount Lebanon governorate; some governments draw a real distinction between this area and the south and border regions, which carry stronger warnings. Check the current, dated advisory for your own country before you go, and again close to your trip: travel.state.gov (US) or gov.uk/foreign-travel-advice (UK).

☼ How to string it together

The National Museum and Sursock Museum sit on opposite sides of the city, so pick one per day rather than rushing both. Downtown's Souks, the mosque and Martyrs' Square are all a few minutes' walk apart and make an easy half-day together. Save the Corniche for late afternoon, when the light and the sunset crowd are both at their best.

Frequently asked questions

What are the must-see sights in Beirut?

The Corniche and Pigeon Rocks for the free seafront view, the National Museum and Sursock Museum for a couple of hours each, and Beirut Souks with the Mohammad Al-Amin Mosque downtown for an easy half-day together. Add Bourj Hammoud for an afternoon that isn't in any museum.

Is the Jeita Grotto, Byblos and Harissa day trip worth it?

Yes — it's the single most popular excursion out of Beirut for a reason, combining a genuinely impressive cave system, a Crusader-era harbor town and a cable-car ride in one day. Budget the whole day for it, and book ahead in the busy summer season.

Do I need to book tours in advance in Beirut?

Mostly no — the National Museum, Sursock Museum, Beirut Souks and the Corniche are all walk-up and self-guided. The exception is the Jeita–Byblos–Harissa day trip, which is worth booking a few days ahead, especially in the busy July–August season.
