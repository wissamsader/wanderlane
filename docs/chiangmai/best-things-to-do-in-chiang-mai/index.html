Best Things to Do in Chiang Mai

The temples that are actually worth it, an ethical elephant day, waterfalls you can climb, and the experiences to book ahead.

You could fill a week here without repeating yourself. This is the honest shortlist — the sights that earn their spot, plus what to book in advance and what to just turn up for.

The temples worth your time

There are hundreds; you don't need to see them all. These five are the ones that stay with you:

The big one

Wat Phra That Doi Suthep

The golden mountain temple above the city — go for sunrise or late afternoon, and for the view over Chiang Mai as much as the shrine. ~30 THB.

See Doi Suthep tours →
Old City

Wat Chedi Luang

A colossal, half-ruined 14th-century pagoda in the heart of the old town. The most atmospheric temple in the city centre.

Old City

Wat Phra Singh

The old town's most revered temple, gleaming gold and Lanna teak, lovely at dusk when the monks chant.

Forest & tunnels

Wat Umong

A 700-year-old forest temple with brick meditation tunnels and a lake, at the foot of Doi Suthep. Quiet, strange, wonderful.

The Silver Temple

Wat Sri Suphan

Entirely hand-worked in silver and aluminium in the Wualai silversmith quarter. Extraordinary up close (note: men only inside the ordination hall).

See elephants — the right way

This matters, so we'll be blunt: don't ride elephants, and skip any 'sanctuary' that offers riding or circus-style shows. Choose observation-and-feeding-only ethical sanctuaries.

☼ The gold standard

Elephant Nature Park is the original ethical sanctuary — a rescue home with no riding, where you observe and help feed. A full day runs around $77 and books out well ahead. If it's full, research any alternative carefully: 'ethical' is a marketing word as often as a real one, and even some certified parks are debated.

✈ Book an ethical elephant day

These sell out days ahead in high season. GetYourGuide and Klook both list the reputable sanctuaries with clear no-riding policies and real reviews.

Into the hills: nature half-days

Free

Sticky Waterfalls (Bua Tong)

Limestone falls you can literally walk up — the rock has grip like velcro. An hour north, completely free, genuinely magic. Best with a scooter or a tour.

See tours →
Thailand's roof

Doi Inthanon

The highest mountain in Thailand — twin pagodas, cloud forest, waterfalls and cool air. A full day trip, best done with a driver.

See Doi Inthanon tours →
Swim

Grand Canyon Chiang Mai

A flooded old quarry turned cliff-jump-and-swim spot with a water park. A hot-afternoon crowd-pleaser south of town.

Time it with a festival, if you can

Yi Peng & Loy Krathong — the lantern festival — is the most beautiful thing that happens here, when thousands of lanterns rise over the city (around November 24–25 in 2026). It's magic, it's crowded, and both accommodation and the ticketed lantern-release events book out months ahead. If your dates line up, plan around it early.

✈ The easy wins to pre-book

Cooking classes, ethical elephant days, Doi Inthanon and Sticky Waterfalls trips, and Yi Peng events are the things that sell out. Everything else you can decide on the day.

Frequently asked questions

What are the must-see temples in Chiang Mai?

Wat Phra That Doi Suthep (the mountain temple with the view), Wat Chedi Luang and Wat Phra Singh in the old city, and the atmospheric forest temple Wat Umong. The Silver Temple, Wat Sri Suphan, is also worth the trip.

Is it ethical to visit elephants in Chiang Mai?

Only at genuine sanctuaries that don't allow riding or performances — Elephant Nature Park is the best-known example, focused on rescue and observation. Avoid any operation offering elephant rides or shows.

What should you book in advance in Chiang Mai?

Cooking classes, ethical elephant sanctuary days, and long day trips (Doi Inthanon, Sticky Waterfalls) — plus everything if you're visiting during the November lantern festival, which sells out the city.
