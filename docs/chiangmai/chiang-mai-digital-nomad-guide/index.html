Chiang Mai Digital Nomad Guide

Where to live, work and café-hop in one of the world's original remote-work hubs — plus SIMs, insurance and the season to avoid.

Chiang Mai has been a remote-work capital for over a decade, and it's easy to see why: fast wifi, a huge café culture, a ready-made community, and a cost of living that lets you actually save. Here's how to set up.

Where to base yourself

Nimman is the nomad heartland — cafés, coworking, restaurants and nightlife in walking distance, and short-term condos with pools and gyms. If you want it cheaper and more local, Santitham is right next door and a fraction of the price. The Old City works too, but it's more tourist-facing. Book a week somewhere central first, then find a monthly condo once you're on the ground.

✈ Find a monthly-friendly place

For a first week while you flat-hunt, book something central and cancellable. Agoda and Booking both list Chiang Mai condos and aparthotels with weekly/monthly rates.

Coworking spaces

Free-ish

CAMP at Maya

A vast café-coworking on top of the Maya mall — free to sit, you just buy drinks (and a code unlocks wifi). Best city view and the easy default.

Serious focus

Punspace

Two quiet, 24-hour locations (Nimman and Tha Phae) built for deep work — from ~289 THB/day. The pick when you need to actually ship.

Community

Yellow Coworking

A long-standing, sociable Nimman-area space with events and a reliable members' crowd — good for meeting people.

Laptop-friendly cafés

Chiang Mai's café scene is world-class and genuinely work-friendly. Ristr8to (award-winning coffee), Graph (tiny and cult), and The Barn are reliable Nimman favourites. Rotate a few — even the great cafés appreciate you not camping on one table for eight hours.

The practical setup

  • Connectivity: grab an eSIM before you land so you're online at the airport, then decide on a local SIM once you're settled. An eSIM is the zero-hassle option for the first week.
  • Cost of living: comfortable is roughly $900–1,400/month (condo, eating out often, coworking); frugal can dip well under that.
  • Insurance: if you're working abroad long-term, get proper travel-medical cover — and if you'll ride a scooter, re-read the engine-size clause (see our scooter guide).
  • Visas: rules change often — check the official Thai e-Visa site for the current tourist-entry and any remote-work options rather than trusting a blog (including this one) on the specifics.

⚠ Plan around burning season

February to April is agricultural burning season and Chiang Mai's air quality can hit genuinely unhealthy levels (AQI 150–300+ in March). Many long-termers simply leave for those weeks. If you're choosing when to base here, aim for roughly October to January.

Frequently asked questions

Is Chiang Mai still good for digital nomads in 2026?

Yes — it remains one of the best-value remote-work bases in the world, with fast wifi, deep café and coworking scenes, an established community and low costs. The main caveat is the Feb–Apr burning season, when many nomads temporarily leave.

How much does it cost to live in Chiang Mai as a nomad?

A comfortable month — central condo, coworking, eating out regularly — runs roughly $900–1,400. Living more like a local in Santitham can bring that well below $900.

What's the best area in Chiang Mai for remote work?

Nimman (Nimmanhaemin) is the nomad hub, with coworking, cafés and condos in walking distance. Santitham next door is cheaper and more local. Both beat the more tourist-oriented Old City for working.

✈ Two things worth sorting before you go

Land already connected with an eSIM instead of hunting for a SIM at the airport, and don't travel uninsured — one medical bill abroad costs many times the premium.
