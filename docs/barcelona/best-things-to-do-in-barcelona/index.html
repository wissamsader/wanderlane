Best Things to Do in Barcelona

The Gaudí sights worth booking weeks ahead, the free wandering that's just as good, and an honest note on visiting a city stretched thin by its own popularity.

You could fill two weeks here without repeating yourself. This is the honest shortlist — the sights that earn the hype, the ones you need to book before you land, and what's worth your time once you're past the postcard five.

The Gaudí sights: book these first

Antoni Gaudí's work is the reason most people come, and the two biggest sights now run timed, capped entry — buy ahead, or you may not get in at all.

Book weeks ahead

Sagrada Família

Gaudí's unfinished basilica is the single most-visited monument in Spain. Timed entry regularly sells out days ahead in high season — this is the one to sort before you land.

Also book ahead

Park Güell

Gaudí's mosaic park has capped entry to the monumental zone at roughly 800 visitors an hour since 2013. Same rule as the Sagrada Família: reserve a timed slot in advance.

The dragon-roof one

Casa Batlló

The more theatrical of Gaudí's two dueling Eixample apartment blocks on Passeig de Gràcia — a rippling, bone-like façade that looks like nothing else on the street.

The wavy stone one

La Pedrera (Casa Milà)

Gaudí's other Passeig de Gràcia masterpiece, a few doors from Casa Batlló — undulating stone, wrought-iron balconies, and a rooftop of surreal chimney sculptures.

The Gothic Quarter, on foot

Barri Gòtic is Barcelona's medieval core, built on the old Roman grid — the cathedral, Plaça Sant Jaume, Plaça Reial, and lanes that tangle enough to get pleasantly lost in. It's genuinely touristy by midday and genuinely atmospheric early morning or after dinner, once the day-trip crowds thin out.

Free, self-guided

Wander it yourself

No booking needed — start at the cathedral and let the lanes take you. Before 9am or after dinner is when it actually feels medieval rather than like a queue.

If you want the history

A guided walking tour

A couple of hours with a guide unlocks the Roman walls and the stories behind the squares that are easy to walk straight past otherwise.

Montjuïc: views, art and the fountain

The hill above the port packs in a castle, one of Spain's best modern-art museums, and — some evenings — a genuinely spectacular free water-and-light show.

Views

Montjuïc Castle & cable car

A hilltop fortress with the best panoramic view of the port and the city, reachable by cable car for the ride itself as much as the destination.

See Montjuïc tours →
Art

Joan Miró Foundation

One of Spain's best modern-art museums, dedicated to the Barcelona-born surrealist — a good indoor option when the sightseeing pace needs a break.

Free, evenings

Magic Fountain (Font Màgica)

A free sound-light-and-water show at the foot of Montjuïc on select evenings. Check the current schedule locally, but it's one of the best free things to do in the city.

La Boqueria — go early

Barcelona's most famous market, just off La Rambla, is genuinely worth seeing — the trick is timing. Go early morning, before the tour groups arrive, and it's still a working market: fishmongers, fruit stalls, a jamón counter slicing to order. By midday it's mostly a photo line. For markets locals actually shop at, our food guide points to Mercat de Sant Antoni and Mercat de la Barceloneta instead.

⚠ Camp Nou: check before you plan around it

FC Barcelona's stadium has been mid-renovation since June 2023, targeting completion around June 2026. The club returned to partial operation in November 2025 at reduced capacity, expanding toward roughly 62,700 seats by March 2026 — and the stadium tour and museum, which normally draw more than 1.2 million visitors a year, are genuinely in flux this season. If a Camp Nou visit matters to your trip, verify the current tour format and availability close to your dates rather than assuming it's business as usual.

Day trips: Montserrat and the Costa Brava

The best reason to leave the city for a day.

The classic day trip

Montserrat

A jagged mountain monastery about an hour out by train plus rack railway or cable car — home to the Black Madonna ('La Moreneta') and a centuries-old boys' choir, with the summit of Sant Jeroni at 1,236m for anyone who wants to hike beyond the monastery itself.

Coast & medieval towns

Costa Brava

Coastal towns a couple of hours northeast — cobbled Girona and the walled beach town of Tossa de Mar are the two names that come up most.

Beach, sailing and a night out

Barceloneta beach is the closest sand to the center — a former fishermen's quarter turned boardwalk, livelier right on the sand and calmer a few blocks inland.

Closest beach

Barceloneta beach

The city's most convenient beach and boardwalk, with seafood chiringuitos along the front. Go a few blocks inland for a quieter, more residential version of the same neighborhood.

Pairs well with the beach

Sunset sailing or catamaran

A couple of hours on the water off Barceloneta is a genuinely nice way to see the skyline from the sea, especially around sunset.

Andalusian, not Catalan

Flamenco show

Worth knowing before you go: flamenco is originally Andalusian, not a Catalan tradition. Barcelona still has established tourist-facing tablaos, among them Tablao Cotton Club and Palau Dalmases, plus shows at Poble Espanyol.

ⓘ An honest note on overtourism

Barcelona is one of Europe's most visible overtourism flashpoints, and it's a live issue, not settled history. The city was already logging more than 9 million annual visitors by 2013; by 2017, surveys found roughly 60% of residents felt the city had reached or exceeded what it could handle. Housing pressure from short-term rentals has been severe enough that the city has moved to phase out short-term tourist-apartment licenses, and Park Güell's visitor cap and active limits on cruise-ship arrivals are part of the same response. None of that means don't come — it means come a little differently: book the timed tickets above so you're not part of an unmanaged crush, treat the residential streets around Park Güell and the Gòtic's courtyards as someone's home rather than a backdrop, spread your sightseeing across a few unhurried days instead of a checklist-sprint, and eat somewhere from our food guide that isn't on the Rambla. It's a better trip anyway.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to book Sagrada Família and Park Güell tickets in advance?

Yes — both routinely sell out days ahead in high season and now run timed, capped entry rather than walk-up access. Book through GetYourGuide before you land, especially for a summer trip.

Is Camp Nou open to visitors right now?

Partially. The stadium has been under major renovation since June 2023 and returned to partial operation at reduced capacity in November 2025. Check the current tour format and availability close to your travel dates rather than assuming normal access.

Is Barcelona too crowded to visit right now?

The hotspots — La Rambla, the Sagrada Família queue, Park Güell at midday — are genuinely strained, but the city is much bigger than its five famous sights. Visiting in May–June or September–October, booking tickets ahead, and spending time in neighborhoods like Gràcia or Sant Andreu goes a long way toward a better trip, for you and the city both.
