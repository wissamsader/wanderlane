Barcelona, the good streets

Beyond the Sagrada Família queues — where locals actually eat and drink, which neighborhood to sleep in, and the tickets worth booking ahead.

Updated July 2026
Home/Barcelona

Barcelona has two menus. There's the one taped to a window on La Rambla, in six languages, next to a photo of paella and a tout holding laminated pages — and there's the one three streets back, chalked on a board, in Catalan only, where the vermut is on tap and nobody's translated anything for you. This guide is about finding the second one.

The real Barcelona eats late, drinks vermut before lunch on weekends, and rarely leaves its own neighborhood to do either. Tapas bars, wine bodegas and vermuterías cluster in Sant Andreu, Gràcia and the back lanes of El Born and El Raval — a few metro stops from the postcard sights, and a different city entirely. Here are the places we'd actually take a friend, starting with the one that's been open since 1820.

El Raval

Bar Marsella

Barcelona's most storied bar, open since 1820 — the original 19th-century interior is still there, and the regulars' list reportedly ran through Picasso, Dalí and Hemingway. Order the house absinthe.

Getting around

  • El Prat airport — Josep Tarradellas Barcelona–El Prat, about 15km southwest of the center and one of Europe's busiest (57.5 million passengers in 2025). Most flights use Terminal 1.
  • Aerobús — the dedicated express bus from T1/T2 to Plaça Catalunya, roughly €6–7 one-way and about 35–40 minutes, running every 5–20 minutes depending on the time of day.
  • Train alternative — the R2 Nord rail line also connects the airport to Barcelona-Sants and Passeig de Gràcia. Cheaper than the Aerobús, less frequent.
  • T-casual card — the current multi-trip metro/bus card, good for 10 journeys and shareable between however many of you are traveling. Zone 1 covers essentially every sight in this guide; buy it at any metro machine and check the current price there, since fares move.
  • On foot — the old-town core (Gòtic, Born, Raval, Eixample-Dreta) is flat and walkable. Notice that most of the places in our eats list above sit outside that core — Sant Andreu, Horta-Guinardó, Sants, Gràcia. That's the point: a short metro ride buys real neighborhood food over Rambla-adjacent tourist pricing.

☼ Best time to visit

May–June and September–October are the sweet spot — warm, without August's peak heat or crowds. Barcelona's holiday season really runs six months, May to October, with August daytime temperatures around 27–31°C; winters are mild, with the thinnest crowds and lowest prices. One honest catch: August is also when a lot of the small, family-run bodegas in this guide close for a few weeks — check hours before building a trip around any single one.

ⓘ Traveling considerately in Barcelona

This isn't abstract here. By 2017, surveys found roughly 60% of Barcelona residents felt the city had reached or exceeded what it could handle from tourism, and housing pressure from short-term rentals has been severe enough that the city has moved to phase out short-term tourist-apartment licenses. Park Güell's visitor cap and active limits on cruise arrivals are part of the same response. None of that means don't come — it means come a little differently: book a hotel rather than a short-term rental where you can, treat the residential streets around Park Güell and the Gòtic's courtyards as someone's home rather than a backdrop, and spend where this guide already points you — Sant Andreu, Gràcia, Horta-Guinardó — rather than only on the Rambla corridor. It's a better trip anyway.

Barcelona FAQ

How many days do you need in Barcelona?

Three full days covers the Gaudí landmarks, the old town, and one proper meal in a couple of neighborhoods. Five to seven is better if you want to add Montserrat, a beach day in Barceloneta, and time to actually sit still at vermut hour instead of rushing it.

What's the best time of year to visit Barcelona?

May–June or September–October — warm and mostly crowd-free, without August's heat spike or the small-business closures that come with it. Winters are mild, quiet and cheap if you don't need beach weather.

Where do locals in Barcelona actually eat and drink?

Mostly outside the postcard core — Sant Andreu, Gràcia, and the back lanes of El Born and El Raval, over tapas and a vermut rather than a Rambla-front menú turístico. Our full food and drink guide has the specific places.
