Where to Stay in Berlin: Best Areas & Hotels

Six neighborhoods that feel like different cities — from Mitte's monuments to Neukölln's Arab Street — and exactly where to book in each.

Berlin is big enough that picking the wrong neighborhood costs you real time — an hour each way on the U-Bahn adds up fast over a long weekend. Six areas cover almost every visitor's needs, and one of them is nothing like what a first-time guide tells you to expect. Here's who each is actually for, and the specific hotels worth booking in each.

First trip and want to walk everywhere → Mitte. Building your trip around the food crawl → Neukölln or Kreuzberg.

Mitte is the safe, obvious choice, and for a first trip it's a genuinely good one. But if the Sonnenallee food crawl in our Berlin food guide is part of your plan, basing yourself in Neukölln or neighboring Kreuzberg turns a special trip across town into a ten-minute walk.

If Sonnenallee is the whole point of your trip

☼ Base yourself near the food

Neukölln puts you inside walking distance of the Arab Street itself; Kreuzberg next door is nearly as close, with its own street-food and bar scene besides. Both beat commuting in from Mitte for an evening crawl. See the full Sonnenallee guide before you pick a hotel.

When to book

  • May through September is Berlin's best-weather window — book two to four weeks ahead in peak summer (July–August), when the mild-weather crowds are thickest.
  • The Berlin Marathon, run through the city in late September, is one of the world's biggest marathons and fills hotels citywide — check the date before you book if you're not running it.
  • Christmas markets (late November–December) bring their own demand despite the cold; book ahead if that's your trip.
  • Winter (December–February) runs genuinely cold, with highs around 3°C and snow common into March — the trade-off is the thinnest crowds and the lowest rates of the year.

Getting from your hotel to the airport

Berlin Brandenburg Airport (BER) sits about 18km southeast of the center. Wherever you stay, the Airport Express (FEX) reaches Hauptbahnhof in roughly 22 minutes, running every 15 minutes; S-Bahn lines S9 and S45 also connect, with S45 conveniently passing through Neukölln on its way in. The station runs 24/7, so a late landing or an early flight out isn't a problem.

Frequently asked questions

Which area of Berlin is best for first-time visitors?

Mitte — it's central, walkable to Brandenburg Gate, Museum Island and the TV Tower, and has the widest range of hotels. It's also the most touristy and most expensive of the six areas, roughly €80–450 a night.

Should I stay in Neukölln instead of Mitte?

Only if the Sonnenallee food crawl (see our food guide) is a real priority, or you want Berlin's best-value neighborhood over walkability to the big monuments. Many visitors split the difference and stay in neighboring Kreuzberg, close to both Neukölln and central Berlin.

How much does a hotel in Berlin cost?

Budget hostel-style rooms — like Heart of Gold's capsules in Mitte — sit at the cheap end of Mitte's roughly €80–450/night range; comfortable mid-range hotels run about €70–170/night in Kreuzberg or Prenzlauer Berg; boutique and upscale stays in Mitte or Charlottenburg run €150–450+/night depending on the season.
