Best Things to Do in Berlin

The monuments worth the walk, the one ticket you must book ahead, and the Cold War history that's more than a photo op.

Berlin's list of essentials is long, spread across a genuinely big city, and complicated by exactly one thing: the best single ticket in town needs to be booked before you land, not after you arrive. Sort that first, and the rest of this list falls into place on foot and by U-Bahn.

Book this first: the Reichstag glass dome

The Reichstag's glass dome is free to enter and gives you a 360° spiral walk over the government quarter and the city beyond — but only with a reserved time slot, and slots fill up. Some dates in 2026 close the dome to visitors entirely, so this is the one thing on this list worth locking in before you plan the rest of your day.

✈ Reserve your Reichstag dome slot

Free entry, but a confirmed time slot is essential — guided Reichstag and government-district tours bundle the reservation with a guide who explains what you're looking at.

The free monuments — no ticket, ever

Three of Berlin's most essential sights cost nothing and are open whenever you show up:

Free, always open

Brandenburg Gate

The city's defining monument, floodlit after dark. Walk past it once during the day and once at night — it reads completely differently both times.

Free, always open

East Side Gallery

1.3km of the Berlin Wall repainted by more than a hundred international artists, right along the river in Friedrichshain. Best walked slowly, early or late to dodge the crowds.

Free, always open

Holocaust Memorial

The Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe — 2,711 concrete slabs of varying height, right by the Brandenburg Gate and Tiergarten. Sobering, and worth the twenty minutes even on a tight schedule.

Museum Island: five museums, one very old island

Museumsinsel is a UNESCO World Heritage Site holding five major museums in one small footprint: the Pergamon, the Neues, the Altes, the Alte Nationalgalerie and the Bode. A combined pass covers all five and saves both money and queueing versus buying tickets one museum at a time.

⚠ Check the Pergamon before you plan around it

The Pergamon Museum's most famous hall — home of the Pergamon Altar — has been closed for a long-term renovation since 2023, with parts of the museum expected to stay closed for years. The rest of Museum Island is unaffected, but confirm what's actually open before you build a day specifically around the Pergamon.

✈ Skip the line on Museum Island

A combined museum pass is the efficient way to see more than one of the five — worth it even if you only manage two museums in an afternoon.

Cold War Berlin: Checkpoint Charlie and the wall that isn't there anymore

Checkpoint Charlie, the most famous Cold War border crossing, is now a small open-air site and museum — worth five minutes on its own, but genuinely comes alive paired with a guided walking tour that explains what actually happened on the ground you're standing on. If Checkpoint Charlie's tourist-market feel undersells it for you, the Berlin Wall Memorial on Bernauer Straße is the quieter, more substantial alternative: a preserved stretch of the former death strip with a documentation center.

Free to view

Checkpoint Charlie

The famous crossing point, reduced to a guardhouse and signage today — the history is in what you bring to it, or in the tour you pair it with.

Book ahead

Consistently one of Berlin's best-selling bookings, and for good reason — a good guide turns scattered plaques and a rebuilt guardhouse into an actual narrative.

The view, and the park underneath it

The Berlin TV Tower (Fernsehturm) at Alexanderplatz has the observation deck and revolving restaurant that give you the skyline view the Reichstag dome can't — time it for sunset if you can. Coming from Brandenburg Gate toward Charlottenburg, cut through Tiergarten, Berlin's answer to Central Park, past the gold-topped Victory Column (Siegessäule) at its center — it turns a transit stop into an actual walk.

After dark: Berghain and the wider club scene

Berlin's techno reputation is real, and Berghain in Friedrichshain is its most famous address — self-described 'world capital of techno,' with a door policy that's near-mythical and entirely unpredictable. You can't book your way past it, and we won't pretend otherwise. The wider clubbing scene in Kreuzberg and Friedrichshain is strong on its own if the door doesn't go your way.

Frequently asked questions

Do I need to book the Reichstag dome in advance?

Yes. Entry is free but only with a reserved time slot, and slots fill up — some 2026 dates close the dome to visitors entirely. Book before you finalize the rest of your day, ideally before you land.

What's the difference between East Side Gallery and Checkpoint Charlie?

East Side Gallery is 1.3km of the actual Berlin Wall, painted by international artists and free to walk anytime. Checkpoint Charlie was a border crossing, not a wall segment — today it's a small guardhouse-and-museum site, best paired with a guided Cold War walking tour for context.

Is the Holocaust Memorial free to visit?

Yes — the Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe is free and open at all times, right by the Brandenburg Gate and the edge of Tiergarten.
