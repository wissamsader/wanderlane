Berlin's food reputation runs on currywurst and döner, and both earn it. But the best Middle Eastern food outside the Middle East itself is a short train ride from the Brandenburg Gate, on one street in Neukölln most visitors never hear about. This is a food crawl down Sonnenallee — Syrian shawarma, Palestinian falafel, Yemeni cooking, Damascene sweets, halal butchers and Levantine grocers, roughly in walking order.

Sonnenallee: how a Beirut street ended up in Neukölln

Berliners have called this street the Arab Street for years — a nickname common enough that Middle East Eye, The National and the South China Morning Post have all covered it. It started in 1975, when Lebanese refugees fleeing the civil war settled North Neukölln; Syrian, Palestinian and Yemeni communities followed in the decades after. Neukölln today has roughly 33,000 Arab residents — about a quarter of Berlin's entire Arab population — and Middle Eastern-origin residents make up close to 18% (around 55,000 people) of the borough.

We know this street because we mapped it ourselves: our own count of roughly 30 businesses on and just off Sonnenallee is one of the densest, most self-contained Arab strips we've seen anywhere — a halal butcher next to a goldsmith next to a falafel counter next to a bridal shop. This guide covers the 15 food, sweets and grocery spots from that list, in the order you'd actually walk them.

Five words worth knowing before you go

  • Shawarma — meat shaved off a vertical spit, wrapped or plated.
  • Falafel — fried chickpea patties, the vegetarian backbone of every menu here.
  • Kunafa (knafeh) — syrup-soaked cheese pastry, usually pistachio-topped.
  • Manakish — Syrian flatbread, often topped with za'atar or cheese.
  • Warbat — a warm, cream-filled pastry, closer to a cream puff than baklava.

Where döner and currywurst fit in

Sonnenallee is the deep cut, but it's part of Berlin's wider street-food identity, not apart from it. Currywurst was invented right here, by Herta Heuwer in 1949, and it's still the sentimental mascot of Berlin street food: sliced sausage, curried ketchup, done in two minutes.

Döner is the bigger story. Berlin-style döner-in-bread — meat, tomato, cucumber, cabbage and sauce folded into flatbread — is widely credited to Kadir Nurman in Kreuzberg in 1972, though the claim is genuinely disputed, with competing stories and a possible earlier Istanbul origin. Whoever invented it, döner is now Berlin's biggest-selling street food, ahead of currywurst — and Sonnenallee's shawarma shops are a close cousin of the same tradition. Order both and you'll taste the family resemblance.

☼ Pair it with the Türkenmarkt

Neukölln's other great food institution is the Maybachufer canal-side market (the 'Türkenmarkt') — Turkish and Middle Eastern produce, cheese, bread and fabric, historically Tuesday and Friday afternoons. Confirm the day before you build a trip around it; if it lines up, it pairs naturally with a Sonnenallee afternoon.

How to actually do the crawl

Sonnenallee has its own S-Bahn station, or take the U7 to Karl-Marx-Straße or Rathaus Neukölln and walk in. Go in the afternoon rather than right after lunch: start with a shawarma or falafel main, walk it off past the goldsmiths, barbershops and the bridal boutique that round out the rest of the strip, then land on kunafa or warbat when you're ready. Two to three hours does it easily, and you'll still have only scratched the fifteen places above — that's rather the point. Go back.

✈ Stay close enough to do this twice

Basing yourself in Neukölln or nearby Kreuzberg puts Sonnenallee a short walk or one stop away, instead of a special trip across town.

Frequently asked questions

What is Sonnenallee, and why is it called Berlin's 'Arab Street'?

Sonnenallee is a street through Neukölln nicknamed 'the Arab Street' by local and international press alike, including Middle East Eye and The National. Its Arab character dates to 1975, when Lebanese civil-war refugees settled North Neukölln; Syrian, Palestinian and Yemeni communities followed. Neukölln now has around 33,000 Arab residents, about a quarter of Berlin's total Arab population.

Is Sonnenallee walkable as a food crawl?

Yes — that's what makes it work. Almost every business here sits directly on Sonnenallee or within a two-block walk. You can genuinely do shawarma, falafel, sweets and a market stop in one afternoon on foot, no transit needed once you're there.

What's the one thing to eat if you only have time for one stop?

Either the shawarma platter at Azzam Restaurant — the flagship of the street, built for sharing — or the falafel at Al Hanan, which multiple reviewers rank among the best in Europe. If you can manage two stops, pair either with kunafa at Malek Al Konafeh to finish.
