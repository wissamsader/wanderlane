Berlin, beyond the wall

The big sights, done efficiently — plus the thing most Berlin guides miss entirely: Sonnenallee, the Levantine food street in the heart of Neukölln.

Updated July 2026
Home/Berlin

Berlin gets sold as Cold War history and techno myths, and both are real. But the thing that actually defines the city right now is a street most visitors never hear about: Sonnenallee, in Neukölln, which Berliners have nicknamed the Arab Street. This guide covers both halves of Berlin — the sights everyone comes for, and the food street almost nobody sends you to.

Here's the part that makes us different: every business we feature in this city sits on or within two blocks of Sonnenallee. Its Arab character goes back to 1975, when Lebanese refugees fleeing the civil war began settling North Neukölln; Syrian, Palestinian and Yemeni communities followed in the decades after. Neukölln today counts roughly 33,000 Arab residents — about a quarter of Berlin's entire Arab population — and it's one of the largest concentrations of Levantine food outside the Middle East itself, all walkable in an afternoon. We go deep on it in our Sonnenallee food crawl; here are seven places to start.

Where to stay

Mitte is the easy first-timer base — Brandenburg Gate, Museum Island and the TV Tower are all walkable or a stop away, though it's the most touristy district too. Kreuzberg brings street art, multicultural food and late-night bars; Neukölln next door is our featured food ground, once overlooked and now genuinely the best-value district for authentic eating (plus Tempelhofer Feld, the old airport-turned-park). Prenzlauer Berg is leafy and family-friendly, Friedrichshain is the nightlife capital with Berlin's cheapest hostel beds, and Charlottenburg is West Berlin elegance with upscale hotels.

Getting around

Berlin Brandenburg Airport (BER) sits about 18km southeast of the center. The Airport Express (FEX) runs every 15 minutes to Hauptbahnhof in roughly 22 minutes, and S-Bahn lines S9 and S45 also connect — S45 conveniently passes through Neukölln and Tempelhof on its way in. The station runs 24/7. Once you're in the city, the BVG network (U-Bahn, S-Bahn, trams and buses) covers everywhere, and Berlin is flat enough to genuinely get around by bike. For Sonnenallee specifically: it has its own S-Bahn station, or take the U7 to Karl-Marx-Straße or Rathaus Neukölln and walk in. Sightseeing hard across several days? The Berlin WelcomeCard bundles unlimited transit with museum and attraction discounts — worth pricing against single tickets before you commit.

☼ Best time to visit

May through September is the sweet spot — summer highs run 22–25°C, and May, September and early October give you nearly the same mild weather as peak summer minus the July–August crowds. Winter runs genuinely cold (highs near 3°C, snow common December–March), though if Christmas markets are your thing, that's a reason to come anyway.

Berlin FAQ

How many days do you need in Berlin?

Three days covers the core sights — Brandenburg Gate, the Reichstag, Museum Island, East Side Gallery — plus a proper afternoon on Sonnenallee for the food crawl. Give it four or five if you want a club night, a full museum day, or a slower pace through Neukölln and Kreuzberg.

What is Sonnenallee, and is it worth a special trip?

Sonnenallee is a street through Neukölln that's been known as Berlin's 'Arab Street' since Lebanese refugees began settling there in 1975, later joined by Syrian, Palestinian and Yemeni communities. Today it's one of the largest concentrations of Levantine food outside the Middle East — shawarma, falafel, kunafa and halal butchers, block after block. It's a short train ride from central Berlin and genuinely worth a dedicated afternoon; see our full food crawl.

Which neighborhood should I stay in for Berlin?

Mitte if it's your first trip and you want to walk to the big sights. Kreuzberg or Neukölln if you want nightlife, street food and a more local feel — Neukölln also puts you steps from Sonnenallee. Charlottenburg is quieter and more upscale, and Friedrichshain has the cheapest hostel beds and the best-known clubs.
