3 Days in Palermo: The Perfect Itinerary

A day-by-day plan that fits the old town, Monreale, the Cathedral, Teatro Massimo and a beach or a Cefalù day trip into three days.

Three days is enough to properly do Palermo: the old town and its markets, the two big Norman set-pieces, and one day trip to close it out. Here's the plan, stop by stop.

☼ Where to sleep for this plan

Base yourself in Kalsa or Politeama/Libertà — both keep everything on Day 1 and Day 2 within walking distance. Full breakdown, with real hotels for every budget, in our where-to-stay guide.

Day 1 — Old town, markets & street food

A day for wandering with no fixed reservations — orient yourself at the crossroads, then eat your way through two markets.

  1. Start at Quattro Canti (Piazza Vigliena), the Baroque crossroads where Palermo's old quarters meet — five minutes, but it orients the rest of your trip.
  2. Walk into Ballarò market for a street-food breakfast: an arancina, pane e panelle, whatever's frying. It runs Monday–Saturday and is loudest by late morning.
  3. Spend early afternoon in Kalsa — Palazzo Abatellis, the Foro Italico seafront, the quieter gallery streets away from the market noise.
  4. Detour into Capo market behind the Cathedral for a quieter, more residential market than Ballarò — a good slow browse before dinner.
  5. Finish in Vucciria after dark: Piazza Garraffello, a drink, more street food. Friday nights bring DJ sets if your dates line up.

For exact places and dishes — including our Michelin Guide-listed flagship — see the full Palermo food guide. For more on both markets as sights in their own right, see what to do in Palermo.

Day 2 — Monreale, the Cathedral & Teatro Massimo

The big set-piece day. Go early for Monreale so you're back in the city with the whole afternoon still ahead.

  1. Morning: bus or taxi out to Monreale Cathedral, about 8 km from the city — budget 2–3 hours there and back, more if you want the cloister too.
  2. Back in Palermo by early afternoon: lunch near the Cassaro or Quattro Canti.
  3. Spend the afternoon at Palermo Cathedral — the royal tombs, the meridian line, the treasury. The Palatine Chapel in the Norman Palace is a short walk away if you have the energy for one more UNESCO stop.
  4. End at Teatro Massimo — catch the late-afternoon light on the facade, take a guided tour, or book an evening performance if the schedule allows.

Day 3 — Mondello beach or a Cefalù day trip

Two good, very different ways to close the trip. Pick by energy level, not guilt — both are the right answer.

VibeBelle Époque sandy bay, easy half-dayNorman cathedral, a beach, and one of Italy's prettiest old towns
Pick this if...You want to relax and be back in the city for a final dinnerYou want one more real 'sight' before you leave

Mondello is reached through La Favorita park — bring the afternoon light and not much of a plan. Cefalù is a fuller day: its own Norman cathedral with Byzantine mosaics, a proper old town, and a beach if you save an hour for the sand.

⚠ Skip the rental car

You won't need one for any of this — Monreale, Cefalù and Mondello are all reachable by bus, train or a booked tour, and Wikivoyage flags Palermo driving directly as "quite dangerous as the rules of the road are not followed." Walk or bus centrally; only rent a car if you're extending the trip further afield.

Frequently asked questions

Is 3 days enough for Palermo?

Yes — three days covers the full historic core, a proper market crawl through Ballarò and Vucciria, Monreale, the Cathedral, Teatro Massimo, and one more day trip or beach day. Add a fourth day if you want both Mondello and Cefalù rather than choosing.

What's the best order to see Palermo's sights in?

Markets and old-town wandering first, while you're still getting oriented; Monreale and the Cathedral together on day two, since they're both morning-to-afternoon commitments; save the beach or Cefalù for last, as the natural wind-down.

Do I need a car for this itinerary?

No. The historic core is walkable, Monreale and Cefalù are both reachable by bus, train or a booked tour, and Palermo's own driving conditions have a well-earned bad reputation. Save the rental car, if you want one, for trips further afield like Segesta.
